My Research

My research focuses on exploring plasma instabilities and turbulence in various magnetized plasma environments through three-dimensional magnetohydrodynamic simulations and observations. By combining theoretical modeling with data analysis, I investigate the fundamental processes that govern plasma behavior in the heliosphere and Earth's magnetosphere.

Research Overview

My doctoral research at Boston University's Center for Space Physics focuses on understanding the complex dynamics of plasma instabilities in the heliosphere. I use advanced three-dimensional magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) simulations to study how Rayleighâ€“Taylor and Kelvinâ€“Helmholtz instabilities develop, interact, and eventually drive turbulence in the heliosheath.

A key aspect of my work involves modeling the heliosphere under realistic interstellar medium conditions. I've developed both single-ion (SI) and multi-ion (MI) MHD models to capture the different behaviors of thermal solar wind ions and hotter pickup ions (PUIs). This multi-fluid approach has revealed new insights into how these particle populations influence instability growth rates and the overall evolution of heliospheric structures.

My research has demonstrated that the nonlinear growth of these instabilities not only drives turbulence but can also lead to dramatic structural changes such as heliotail separation. These findings improve our understanding of large-scale plasma evolution and have implications for interpreting observations from missions like Voyager and IBEX.

Past Research Projects

Solar Wind Dynamic Pressure Effects on Magnetospheric Electrons

Completed

Investigated the impact of sudden drops in solar wind dynamic pressure on energetic electrons in Earth's inner magnetosphere using satellite observations and statistical analysis. Demonstrated that electron phase space density is a key driver of flux variations during dynamic pressure decreases.

Institution: Peking University (Master's Research)
Supervisor: Prof. Qiugang Zong
Duration: September 2018 - March 2020

Warm Plasma Cloak Dynamics in the Magnetosphere

Completed

Developed neural network models to forecast plasma distributions from satellite time-series data, achieving up to 97% accuracy in quiet conditions and 90% in disturbed conditions. Created 2D spatial models of warm plasma cloak particle distributions.

Institution: UCLA (Visiting Research)
Supervisor: Prof. Jacob Bortnik
Duration: July 2019 - September 2019

Substorm Response to Interplanetary Shocks

Completed

Conducted statistical analysis of interplanetary magnetic field pre-conditions during substorms and validated the impact of interplanetary shocks on the Auroral Electrojet index and electron dynamics at geosynchronous orbit.
