Research Overview

My doctoral research in Professor Merav Opher's group at Boston University and the SHIELD (Solar-wind with Hydrogen Ion Exchange and Large-scale Dynamics) Drive Center focuses on plasma instabilities and turbulence in the heliosheath. I contributed to developing a three-dimensional MHD model with block-adaptive mesh refinement on HPC clusters, collaborating with the University of Michigan. I revealed that charge exchange forces drive Rayleigh-Taylor instability while velocity shear triggers Kelvin-Helmholtz instability and demonstrated their periodic evolution from coupled growth to decoupling phases. I further collaborated with University of Alabama to develop power spectra and kurtosis workflows to track energy cascade from large-scale instabilities to fully developed turbulence.

During my master's and undergraduate research at Peking University, I conducted statistical analysis of multi-satellite data investigating magnetospheric responses to solar wind variations, developing automated event detection algorithms and applying Superposed Epoch Analysis. This resulted in two first-author publications in Journal of Geophysical Research: Space Physics and provided practical experience with large-scale observational datasets applicable to solar remote-sensing data.

Current Research Projects

1. Triggering Conditions of Instabilities in the Heliosheath

Ongoing NASA Fellowship Funded

To understand the triggering mechanisms of RT/KH instabilities in the heliosheath, I participated in constructing a three-dimensional multi-fluid parallel MHD model of the solar system boundary, incorporating block-adaptive mesh refinement technology to achieve efficient numerical simulation of complex plasma processes. Building on this foundation, I innovatively adopted a progressive modeling strategy of "coarse-grid steady-state followed by fine-grid time-domain" to precisely analyze the origins of instabilities in the heliosheath.

Key Findings:

  • First revealed that magnetic tension and charge exchange can synergistically decelerate the solar wind, forming a unique and stable low-speed region
  • This low-speed region establishes the necessary physical conditions for subsequent instability development
  • Developed novel numerical techniques combining adaptive mesh refinement with progressive grid resolution strategies

Methods:

Three-dimensional multi-fluid MHD simulations using BATS-R-US code with block-adaptive mesh refinement, progressive coarse-to-fine grid modeling, collaboration with University of Michigan on HPC implementation.

Duration: 2020 - Present
Collaborators: Prof. Merav Opher (BU), Dr. Marc Kornbleuth (BU), University of Michigan HPC Team
Publications: Ma et al. 2024 (ApJ, IF 5.4, Q1)

2. Coupling and Evolution Mechanisms of Instabilities in the Heliosheath

Ongoing NASA Fellowship Funded

To investigate the generation mechanisms and evolutionary patterns of RT and KH instabilities in the heliosheath, I constructed an automated multi-dimensional simulation analysis pipeline, including spatial slicing, time-series extraction, and quantitative growth rate fitting methods to systematically verify theoretical model predictions. This research breaks from previous limitations that focused only on the heliopause, extending our view deep into the heliosheath interior.

Key Findings:

  • Clarified the physical mechanisms of two types of instabilities: charge exchange forces in the density gradient direction drive RT instability, while velocity shear in the solar wind low-speed region triggers KH instability
  • Through long-timescale evolution simulations, revealed the periodic evolutionary characteristics from initial coupled growth to later decoupling phases
  • Demonstrated that these instabilities jointly reshape solar wind structure and magnetic field topology, playing a crucial role in the long-term evolution and gradual opening of the heliotail

Methods:

Automated multi-dimensional analysis workflows including spatial slicing, time-series extraction, quantitative growth rate fitting, long-duration 3D MHD simulations tracking full evolutionary cycles.

Duration: 2020 - Present
Collaborators: Prof. Merav Opher (BU), Dr. Marc Kornbleuth (BU)
Publications: Ma et al. 2024 (ApJ, IF 5.4, Q1), Ma et al. 2025 (ApJ, IF 5.4, Q1)

3. Cross-Scale Energy Cascade Mechanisms in Magnetohydrodynamic Turbulence

Ongoing NASA Fellowship Funded

Addressing the key scientific question of how large-scale instabilities in the heliosheath develop into small-scale turbulence, I further utilized the MHD simulation results to construct computational workflows for magnetic field power spectra and kurtosis, quantitatively characterizing the temporal evolution of turbulent energy cascade and intermittency.

Key Findings:

  • First established a continuous physical picture from large-scale instabilities to small-scale turbulence in the heliosheath interior
  • Revealed the complex coupling between magnetic field structures and plasma dynamics
  • Provided new microphysical foundations for predicting the energy spectra and distribution of energetic neutral atoms in the heliosphere

Methods:

Power spectral analysis of magnetic field fluctuations, kurtosis calculations to quantify intermittency, collaboration with University of Alabama on turbulence analysis workflows.

Duration: 2022 - Present
Collaborators: Prof. Merav Opher (BU), Dr. Marc Kornbleuth (BU), University of Alabama Collaboration
Publications: Manuscript in preparation for The Astrophysical Journal

Past Projects

Solar Wind Dynamic Pressure Effects on Magnetospheric Electrons

2018 - 2020 | Peking University

Conducted statistical studies of energetic electron drift echoes and phase space density dynamics in Earth's magnetosphere in response to solar wind dynamic pressure variations. Developed automated event detection algorithms and applied Superposed Epoch Analysis techniques.

Statistical Analysis Van Allen Probes Magnetosphere Superposed Epoch Analysis

Warm Plasma Cloak Dynamics and Forecasting

2019 | UCLA (Visiting Scholar)

Developed neural network models for forecasting plasmaspheric electron density and explored 2D spatial modeling of the warm plasma cloak. Achieved 97% accuracy for quiet periods and 90% for disturbed periods.

Machine Learning Neural Networks Plasmasphere

Substorm Response to Interplanetary Shocks

2016 - 2018 | Peking University

Performed statistical analysis of intense substorm occurrences following interplanetary shock impacts, examining the role of IMF orientation and solar wind conditions in triggering magnetospheric substorms. Applied automated event detection and statistical methods.

Substorms IMF Statistical Study
